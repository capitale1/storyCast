Women Remaking Kigali's Art Scene

African Art Documentary

In Kigali, a quiet transformation is unfolding. A new generation of women artists is reshaping the city's creative identity — turning art into voice, livelihood, and community. At the centre of this shift is Jemima Kakizi, founder of the Impudu Art platform, and a growing collective of women artists who are no longer waiting for space. They are creating it.

About This Documentary

This short documentary follows women artists in Kigali as they navigate a rapidly changing city and an art world that has historically marginalised them. Through the work of Jemima Kakizi's Impudu Art platform, they are finding not just visibility, but livelihood.

"Africa doesn't need to be saved. Africa needs to be heard. In Kigali, art is no longer just expression. It is structure. It is livelihood. It is community being rebuilt."

The film features artist Sega Zeidi Irago, who builds portraits from tufted yarn; curator Jemima Kakizi, who founded Impudu Art after struggling to make a living from her own practice; and Nigerian curator Ademola Adesina, who observes the shift from the outside. Together they document a moment where African women artists are not just participating in the art world — they are reshaping it.

On visibility and livelihood

A recurring theme in the documentary is the gap between making art and making a living from it. Kakizi's platform was born from personal experience — the difficulty of sustaining a practice as a woman artist in a market that did not yet value her work. Her goal now is practical as much as cultural: when she sees artists able to make a living from their art, that's the first measure of success.

But it all started because it was also hard for me to get opportunities as a woman artist and make a living from my art. I thought that coming together would give us more visibility. And also when I started doing art, I think I knew like three women who were practicing, which is a very small number.
